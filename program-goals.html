Program Goals


Context

According to the U.S. Department of Justice Civil Rights Division and U.S. Department of Education Office for Civil Rights, an increasing number of Asian American and Pacific Islander (AAPI) students have reported experiencing “bullying and harassment by classmates because of their race or national origin, including their ethnicity, ancestry, and language” (U.S. Department of Justice, 2021). Recent reviews have found little to no scholarship on or implementation of Bystander Intervention curriculum in schools, which represent “both… places of work for teachers and staff and… as places of education for students” in most Westernized societies (Monnin, 2021, p. 1).

Aims

The proposed curriculum will teach K-12 educators how to respond to COVID-19 related harassment in a school environment, and will consist of two main units with one focused on delivering instruction in bystander intervention strategies, and the other focused on equipping learners with techniques to respond to COVID-19 related harassment if they experience it in the course of their day-to-day work as educators. Each major section of this design blueprint will play an important role in the needs analysis, evaluation protocols, development, piloting, and eventual implementation stages of the curriculum.

Scope

To clarify the scope within a specific context, curriculum developers will define COVID-19 related harassment as:

    Harassment, discrimination, and/or disrespectful behaviors towards individuals including but not limited to the basis of race, national origin, ethnicity, ancestry,and language abilities. The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 has disproportionately affected those who identify as Asian American, Native Hawaiian, and Pacific Islander (AANHPI);
    Harassment, discrimination, and/or disrespectful behaviors towards individuals based on their decision to wear or not wear a mask, to obtain or not obtain vaccinations, and other areas of potential contention.

Long Term Impact

Ultimately, the proposed curriculum also aims to pave the way for a youth-adapted curriculum for children from 3 to 18 years old as part of a larger initiative called “The 5Ds for Kids”, envisioned with becoming the “Stop, Drop, and Roll” of the current generation and for many generations to come.
